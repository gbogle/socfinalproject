01

What is medicalization?

Sociologist Peter Conrad defines medicalization as the process by which non-medical problems become defined and treated as medical conditions. This is usually seen in terms of illness or disorder (Conrad, 2007). Throughout history, this process has been driven by shifting "engines": first the medical profession itself, then biotechnology companies and pharmaceutical industries, and more recently, consumers seeking diagnoses.

What makes medicalization powerful is that it is not neutral. When a behavior or experience becomes a medical condition, it changes how we think about it, who has authority over it, and what solutions we reach for. Masturbation, homosexuality, and "nymphomania" were all once medical diagnoses. All of them reflect the cultural politics of their era far more than any biological reality.

"Medicalization describes a process by which nonmedical problems become defined and treated as medical problems, usually in terms of illness and disorders."

Conrad's framework identified three modern engines of medicalization: the medical profession, the pharmaceutical and biotechnology industries, and consumer demand. But his analysis predates the social media era. A fourth engine has emerged, and it fits in your pocket.

Medicalization

The process by which human conditions are redefined as medical problems requiring treatment. Not always wrong, but never politically neutral.

02

Diagnoses are socially constructed

Conrad and Barker (2010) argue that illness categories are not simply discovered in nature. They are culturally produced. Medical knowledge is always shaped by the social contexts in which it is created: who has power, what counts as evidence, whose experiences get named and legitimized.

This doesn't mean mental health conditions aren't real or that suffering isn't genuine. It means the categories we use to describe that suffering reflect particular historical, cultural, and institutional choices. The boundary between "quirky" and "ADHD," between "sensitive" and "BPD," is drawn by people, not by biology alone.

When a teenager watches a video and thinks "that's exactly me", they are not discovering a pre-existing diagnosis. They are participating in the social construction of their own illness identity.

TikTok and Instagram accelerate this process at a scale no medical institution ever could. When a single "signs you have ADHD" video reaches 40 million views, the virality shapes how millions of people interpret their own minds.

Course Concept

Social Construction of Illness

Illness categories are produced by social and cultural forces, not simply discovered through neutral science.

Key Source

Conrad & Barker (2010)

Argues that medical knowledge is always shaped by power, culture, and context with real consequences for who gets labeled and how.

03

The algorithm as a new engine of medicalization

Conrad identified three historical engines of medicalization: the medical profession, the pharmaceutical and biotechnology industries, and consumers. Social media platforms represent a fourth engine, which is one Conrad never theorized because it didn't exist.

Karizat et al. (2021) show that TikTok users develop "algorithmic folk theories". This is described as informal understandings of how the platform's recommendation system works, and actively co-produce their own identities through this process. When a user engages with one ADHD video, the algorithm surfaces more. The user starts to see themselves reflected back.

The algorithm doesn't know you have ADHD. It knows you kept watching ADHD content, and because of this, it shows you more.

1

The Medical Profession

Doctors and psychiatrists historically held authority to define what counts as a disorder. Their power to name and label was the primary engine of medicalization in the 20th century.

2

Biotechnology & Pharma

Drug companies expanded diagnostic categories to grow markets. Conditions like "social anxiety disorder" gained traction partly because medications existed to treat them.

3

Consumer Demand

Patients began seeking diagnoses themselves through internet searches, patient advocacy groups, and direct-to-consumer advertising.

4

The Algorithm ← new

Recommendation systems on TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube now function as a fourth engine that contribute to surfacing diagnostic content at massive scale. In turn, this works by reinforcing identity through repetition, and creating feedback loops between self-perception and algorithmic output.

04

Online quizzes specifically designed without neutrality

The symptom checker you just completed was a parody, but it wasn't far from the real thing. Dozens of websites offer free "ADHD tests," "autism screenings," and "BPD assessments" that look clinical but are designed to maximize engagement, not diagnostic accuracy.

Davis and Chouinard (2016) argue through affordance theory that designed objects not only enable behaviors, but also request them. A quiz format with a progress bar, official-sounding language, and a "result" at the end is not neutral. It is built to guide users toward a conclusion. The design itself is an argument: you have something. Here is what it is.

The progress bar filling toward a diagnosis isn't a neutral interface feature desgined only to show progress. This design cultivates an experience that makes medicalization feel inevitable.

This is medicalization as dark pattern. Interface design choices use the visual grammar of medical authority with clean layouts, clinical language, percentage scores to lend legitimacy to what are essentially engagement tools.

Key Concept

Affordances

The features of a designed object that suggest or "request" specific uses. A quiz with a progress bar affords and nudges completion and result-seeking.

Additional Source

Karizat et al. (2021)

TikTok users co-produce identity through algorithmic interactions. For example, the platform shapes self-perception through what it shows you about yourself.

05

Why this matters, and why it's complicated

This is not an argument that self-diagnosis is always wrong, or that ADHD and autism aren't real. Many people, especially women, people of color, and queer individuals, have been systematically underdiagnosed by a medical system that wasn't built to see them. Online communities and viral content have genuinely helped people access language for experiences they couldn't name.

But the sociology of medicalization asks us to think carefully about who benefits when more people identify as disordered. Pharmaceutical companies benefit. Platforms benefit, as mental health content drives enormous engagement. And sometimes the individuals benefit too: a diagnosis can mean community, accommodations, and self-understanding.

The problem is not that medicalization happens. The problem is that algorithmic medicalization happens invisibly, and is shaped by engagement logic rather than clinical judgment. Additionally, this occurs at a scale and speed no medical institution could match, to people who believe they are receiving objective information when they are in fact experiencing a designed process.

The quiz felt medical. The algorithm felt like a mirror. Neither was neutral. Both were built.
